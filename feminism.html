STEM and Feminism

Understanding the implications of the statistics of women in STEM, and the importance of the issue in terms
of feminism.

Background Image Source: Medium

Combat Harmful Stereotypes Against Women

Stereotypically, women are "more emotional" and "worse at logical thinking," which discourages them from the STEM field as it requires a lot of logical thinking, not to mention STEM is a stress-heavy field (emotional is a no-no). The lack of representation of women in STEM, as shown by the statistics, perpetuates the stereotype that women are not good enough for STEM.

This acts almost like a negative feedback loop, which further discourages young women from pursuing STEM as they are made to believe they are born such that their gender disqualifies them from the STEM field (or gives them a natural disadvantage). In turn, the statistics of women in STEM are kept low.

Concerns Women's Equality, Especially Women of Color

Despite the fact that jobs in the STEM field pays more than jobs in the other field, women working in STEM are still, overall, being paid less than men.

Women are less likely to obtain job opportunities in the STEM field as well since men are more favored in the field than women due to the stereotypes.

This is not to mention women of color, who constitute a small percentage of STEM occupations and have, overall, less equal pay compared to white women.

The Issue Affects Women of All Ages

Children, Students (Kids/Teens/College), Adults

The Issue Affects Children

The stereotype that women are not qualified for the STEM field is perpetuated and ingrained in the mind of women (and men) since a young.

Children are more likely to be influenced when they are young. Parents, family members, and teachers may implictly enforce harmful stereotypes of women onto their children when they're young - young girls are expected to be perfect ("good girls") and to not take risks, while boys are encouraged the opposite (to take more risks) as "boys will be boys," (Learn more: Camilla Lundin TED Talk - Why you shouldn't be a "good girl") and henceforth they would be held less accountable for their actions.

Young girls likely to be told that they are not fit for any STEM-related fields as they are "more emotional" and therefore "worse at logical thinking", which is important in the STEM field.

The Issue Affects Students (Kids/Teens/College)

STEM fields, unlike the arts field, is less forgiving - there is only a single answer to a math problem. A single failure for a young girl in STEM, may discourage her from pursuing the path ever again.

Young girls and women who do want to pursue the field may face opposition from their family due to the stereotypes and, therefore, disbelief that women would do well in the field.

Additionally, due to the lack of women's representation in STEM, young girls most likely would not have role models who can give them confidence and motivation to pursue a STEM-field degree and/or career. They may also be, therefore, encouraged to leave the STEM field.

The Issue Affects Adults

Women would have a lower chance of getting a STEM-related job if the interviewer or HR team holds the belief that women would not be a good candidate for their company solely due to their gender.

Additionally, women still receive unequal pay in the STEM field - “an entry-level salary for a man is more than $4,000 higher than what's paid to a woman with comparable credentials.” (Stanford Graduate School of Business)
